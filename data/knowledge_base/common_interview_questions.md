# Common Interview Questions and How to Answer Them

## "What are your greatest strengths?"
Pick two or three strengths that are genuinely yours and directly relevant to the role,
then back each with a brief, specific example and result rather than just listing traits.
Read the job description to choose strengths that match what they need. Concrete evidence
("I'm strong at stakeholder communication — I ran the weekly cross-team sync that cut
missed handoffs by half") is far more convincing than adjectives alone.

## "What is your greatest weakness?"
Name a real weakness, not a disguised strength like "I work too hard", and then show what
you are actively doing to improve it. Choose something that is not central to the core of
the job, and keep the focus on your self-awareness and growth. Honesty plus a concrete
improvement plan reads as maturity; a fake weakness reads as evasive.

## "Why do you want to work here?"
Show you have researched the company and connect something specific about it, its mission,
product, values, or a recent development, to your own goals and what you bring. Avoid
generic answers that could apply to any employer. This question tests genuine interest, so
specificity about why this company and this role is what sets a strong answer apart.

## "Why are you leaving your current job?" / "Why did you leave?"
Stay positive and forward-looking. Frame it around what you are moving toward, such as
growth, new challenges, or better alignment with your goals, rather than badmouthing your
current or former employer, which reads as a red flag. Even if the real reason is
negative, phrase it professionally and focus on the opportunity ahead.

## "Where do you see yourself in five years?"
Show ambition and direction without sounding rigid or implying you will quickly leave.
Connect your goals to growth that this role and company could realistically support, and
emphasize developing skills and taking on more responsibility. Interviewers use this to
gauge motivation and whether your path fits the role, not to hold you to a literal plan.

## "What are your salary expectations?"
Where possible, give a researched range based on the role, your experience, and the
location rather than a single number, or turn it around by asking their budgeted range. If
pressed early, a range keeps you flexible. Avoid being the first to commit to an exact
figure when you can, and anchor any number to market data.

## "Do you have any questions for us?"
Always say yes and ask thoughtful questions. Good ones cover what success looks like in the
first 6-12 months, the team's biggest current challenges, how performance is measured, the
team culture and working style, and next steps in the process. Avoid leading with salary,
time off, or perks in early rounds. Strong questions signal genuine interest and help you
evaluate the role.

## "Tell me about yourself."
Use a present-past-future structure: your current role and a relevant strength, the past
experience that led you here, and why this role is the logical next step. Keep it to about
a minute, keep it professional and role-relevant, and end by handing the conversation
back. It is usually the opener, so prepare and practice it specifically.

## How should I handle a question I don't know how to answer?
It is fine to pause and think, or to ask for a moment to gather your thoughts. If you truly
do not know, be honest, then show how you would approach finding the answer rather than
bluffing. For technical or problem-solving questions, talk through your reasoning out loud,
since interviewers often care more about how you think than about a perfect answer.

## How do I talk about being fired or laid off?
Be honest, brief, and unemotional. For a layoff, note it was a business decision (role
eliminated, restructuring) and pivot to what you are looking for next. If you were fired,
state it simply, take appropriate responsibility without oversharing, focus on what you
learned, and move the conversation forward. Dwelling, blaming, or lying makes it worse;
a calm, growth-oriented framing defuses it.
