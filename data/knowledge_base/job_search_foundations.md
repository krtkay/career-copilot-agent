# Job Search Foundations and Application Strategy

## How do I start a job search?
Start by getting clear on what you are looking for: the kind of role, level, industry,
location or remote preference, and your must-haves versus nice-to-haves. Then prepare your
core materials, a strong master resume and an up-to-date LinkedIn profile, before applying
widely. Define a realistic target of roles and companies that fit, and set up a simple
system to track applications. Clarity up front prevents scattered, low-quality applying
later and lets you tailor effectively.

## What is the "hidden job market"?
Many roles are filled through referrals, internal moves, and networking before or instead
of being widely advertised, which is often called the hidden job market. This is why
relying only on public job boards misses a large share of opportunities, and why
networking and informational conversations are so valuable: they surface roles early and
get you referred. Aim to combine applying to advertised roles with proactively building
relationships in your target companies and field.

## How do I read a job description effectively?
Treat the job description as a map of what to emphasize. Separate the true requirements
from the long wish list, since most postings list more than any single hire will have, and
you generally should apply if you meet the core requirements even if not every "preferred"
item. Note the repeated skills, tools, and keywords, and mirror that language truthfully in
your resume and cover letter. Use the responsibilities to anticipate interview questions
and to prepare relevant examples.

## Should I apply if I don't meet every requirement?
Usually yes, if you meet most of the core requirements. Job postings describe an ideal
candidate who rarely exists, and many strong hires did not tick every box. Research
commonly cited suggests some groups are more likely to self-select out when they do not
meet every listed criterion, which can mean missing roles they would have gotten. Focus on
whether you can do the core of the job and make a compelling case for the gap through
transferable skills, learning ability, and relevant results.

## How do I stay organized during the search?
Keep a tracker with each company, role, application date, status, contacts, and next
steps, so nothing slips and you follow up on time. Set a sustainable routine with weekly
process goals you control, such as a target number of tailored applications and networking
touches, rather than only outcome goals like offers. Batch similar tasks, keep copies of
each tailored resume and the job description you applied with, and review what is working
every couple of weeks to adjust.

## How do I handle rejection and stay motivated?
Rejection is a normal, high-frequency part of job searching, and most applications do not
convert, so do not read every "no" as a verdict on your worth. Treat each interview as
practice that compounds, ask for feedback when you can, and protect your energy with
routine, breaks, and support from others. Focus on the inputs you control. Persistence and
steady, targeted effort, combined with networking, are what eventually produce offers.

## How do I follow up after applying?
If you have a contact at the company, a brief, polite follow-up a week or two after
applying is reasonable and can help, but avoid repeated or pushy messages. After an
interview, always send a thank-you within a day. If a recruiter gave a timeline, wait
until it passes before checking in. Respectful, well-timed follow-up signals genuine
interest; over-messaging signals the opposite.
