# Behavioral Interviews and the STAR Method

## What are behavioral interview questions?
Behavioral questions ask you to describe how you handled real past situations, on the
premise that past behavior predicts future performance. They usually begin with "Tell
me about a time when..." or "Give me an example of...". Common ones include a time you
faced a conflict, made a mistake, met a tight deadline, led a project, or had to
persuade someone. Interviewers use them to assess skills like problem-solving,
leadership, collaboration, and resilience with evidence rather than opinion.

## What is the STAR method?
STAR is a framework for structuring behavioral answers so they are clear and complete.
It stands for Situation (briefly set the context), Task (explain your specific
responsibility or the goal), Action (describe the steps you personally took), and
Result (share the outcome, ideally quantified). The Action section should be the bulk
of your answer, roughly 60% of it, because interviewers care most about what you
personally did. Keep Situation and Task brief, and always finish with a concrete
Result, since candidates most often forget that part.

## How do I build strong STAR stories?
Prepare three to five flexible stories in advance rather than scripting a separate
answer for every possible question, because one good story can be adapted to several
questions. Review the job description for the skills it emphasizes and choose examples
that demonstrate those skills. Use "I" rather than "we" so your individual contribution
is clear, and vary your examples across different parts of your experience. Practice
them out loud so they sound natural, and aim for roughly one to two minutes per answer.
Do not memorize word for word, which sounds robotic and breaks down if the question is
phrased differently than you expected.

## How do I answer "Tell me about a time you failed"?
Choose a real failure with a genuine lesson, not a humble-brag disguised as a weakness.
Use STAR: briefly describe the situation and what went wrong, own your part honestly
without blaming others, then spend most of the answer on what you did in response and
what you changed afterward. End on the result and the lesson, showing growth. Honesty
about a real failure with a clear takeaway signals integrity and self-awareness, which
interviewers value more than a flawless record.

## How should I answer "Tell me about yourself"?
This opener is not an invitation to recite your whole life. Use a present-past-future
structure: start with your current role and a relevant strength, briefly connect the
past experience that got you here, then pitch why this role is the logical next step.
Keep it to about a minute, keep it professional and relevant to the job, and finish by
handing the conversation back to the interviewer. It sets the tone, so prepare and
practice it specifically.

## What questions should I ask the interviewer?
Always prepare a few thoughtful questions, because having none signals disinterest. Ask
about what success looks like in the role in the first six to twelve months, the
biggest challenges the team is facing, how performance is evaluated, and the team's
working style. Avoid leading with questions about salary, time off, or perks in an early
interview. Good questions double as a way for you to assess whether the role is right
for you.
