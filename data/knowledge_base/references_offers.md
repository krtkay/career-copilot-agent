# References, Offers, and Background Checks

## How do I choose and prepare references?
Choose people who have directly seen your work and will speak specifically and positively
about you, typically former managers, then senior colleagues, clients, or mentors, rather
than friends or family. Aim for three to four. Always ask permission before listing
someone, and give each reference a heads-up when a check is coming, along with the role
you are applying for and a reminder of relevant accomplishments so they can speak to what
matters most. Keep a separate references document with each person's name, title,
relationship to you, and contact details, and provide it only when asked rather than
putting "references available on request" on your resume.

## When are references and background checks done?
References and background checks usually come late in the process, typically after a
strong interview or once an offer is on the table, so you do not need to hand them over
early. A background check may verify employment history, education, and, depending on the
role and local law, criminal records or credit. Be honest throughout your application and
interviews, because discrepancies between what you claimed and what a check finds are a
common reason offers are withdrawn. If there is something in your history that may surface,
it is usually better to address it proactively and honestly.

## How should I handle a job offer?
When you receive an offer, express genuine enthusiasm and thanks, then ask for the offer
in writing and for reasonable time to consider it rather than accepting on the spot. Read
the full written offer carefully, including base salary, bonus, equity, benefits, start
date, title, and any conditions. It is normal and acceptable to ask for a few days to
decide. Use that time to evaluate the whole package against your priorities and market
research, and to negotiate if appropriate. Do not resign from your current job until you
have the final, agreed offer in writing.

## How do I compare multiple offers?
Evaluate each offer on the whole package, not just base salary: consider bonus and equity,
benefits, pension or retirement contributions, paid time off, work location and
flexibility, the role's growth potential, the team and manager, the company's stability,
and commute or relocation. Weigh these against your own priorities, which you should
decide before comparing. A slightly lower salary can be the better offer if growth,
learning, culture, or flexibility matter more to you. Try to compare total compensation
and long-term prospects, not headline numbers alone.

## How do I decline an offer or resign gracefully?
If you decline an offer, do it promptly, politely, and in writing, thanking them for their
time and the opportunity, without burning bridges, since you may cross paths again. To
resign from a current role, give appropriate notice (commonly two weeks, or per your
contract), tell your manager directly and first, provide a short written resignation, and
offer to help with a smooth handover. Stay positive and professional through your notice
period; a graceful exit protects your reputation and your references.

## Should I accept a counteroffer from my current employer?
When you resign, your current employer may make a counteroffer to keep you. Weigh it
carefully rather than reacting emotionally. Consider why you were leaving in the first
place, since a raise rarely fixes issues like limited growth, poor management, or
misalignment, and whether accepting could affect trust going forward. A counteroffer can
occasionally be the right choice, but if the reasons you started looking still stand, more
money alone usually does not resolve them.
