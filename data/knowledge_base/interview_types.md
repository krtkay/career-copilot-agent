# Interview Types: Phone Screens, Video, and Panels

## What is a phone screen and how do I prepare?
A phone screen is usually the first interview, a short call (often 20-30 minutes) with a
recruiter or HR to check that your background matches the role and that you communicate
well before investing in a full interview. It is a real filter that eliminates a large
share of candidates, so treat it as seriously as an in-person round. Prepare a quiet,
private space with good signal, confirm the date, time, and who is calling whom, and
keep your resume, the job description, and a short "cheat sheet" of your key achievements
and questions in front of you. If a recruiter calls unexpectedly from an unknown number
while you are unprepared or somewhere noisy, it is fine to let it go to voicemail and
call back promptly from a quiet place.

## How do I make a good impression on a phone screen?
Because the interviewer cannot see you, your voice carries everything, so keep your tone
upbeat and genuine from start to finish; interviewers can often "hear your smile", and
many candidates start strong and fade. Answer concisely and stay on topic, since
rambling is the most common phone-screen mistake. Tell specific, plain-English stories
about what you accomplished rather than jargon. Have a prepared "Tell me about yourself"
summary of about a minute, and be ready for common questions like why you want the role,
what you know about it, and your salary expectations. Standing up and smiling while you
talk genuinely improves how you sound. Close by thanking them and asking what the next
step is.

## How is a video interview different, and how do I prepare?
A video interview adds visual presence, so preparation covers both content and setup.
Test your camera, microphone, internet connection, and the platform in advance, and have
a backup plan such as a phone number in case the connection drops. Choose a quiet,
well-lit space with a clean, uncluttered background, position the camera at eye level,
and look at the camera rather than the screen so you appear to make eye contact. Dress as
you would for an in-person interview. You can keep brief notes on sticky notes near the
camera, but do not read from a script. Sit up, and let a little extra energy come
through, since enthusiasm reads as flatter on video than in person.

## What is a one-way (recorded) video interview?
Some employers use a one-way video platform where you record answers to preset questions
rather than talking to a live person. You usually get a short, fixed time to think and
then to answer, and sometimes a limited number of re-records. Practice common questions
within a time limit beforehand, outline your key points during the prep time rather than
scripting a full answer, and speak clearly without rushing. Follow the platform's
instructions for equipment and submission, and check how much time and how many attempts
you get per question.

## What is a panel interview and how do I handle it?
In a panel interview several people interview you at once, often from different functions.
Get everyone's names and roles at the start if you can, and address the person who asked
each question while still making eye contact with the others as you answer. Treat every
panelist as important regardless of seniority, since any of them may weigh in on the
decision. Prepare a question or two you can direct to different members. Afterward, send
a thank-you note to each person where possible, or one note that acknowledges the group.

## How long do interviews take and how many rounds are there?
Processes vary, but a common path is a recruiter phone screen, then one or more rounds
with the hiring manager and team (by video or on-site), sometimes including a skills
exercise or a final conversation with a senior leader. Phone screens average around 30
minutes; later rounds run longer. Ask the recruiter what the full process looks like so
you can plan, and always ask at the end of each round what the next step and rough
timeline are.
