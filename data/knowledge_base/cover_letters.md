# Cover Letters and Outreach

## Do I still need a cover letter?
Often yes. Many applications still request one, and when they do, a strong cover letter
can meaningfully help, while a generic or careless one can hurt. When an application
makes it optional, a tailored letter is usually worth writing for roles you genuinely
want, because it lets you connect your experience to the specific job and company in a
way a resume cannot. If a posting says no cover letter, respect that.

## How should a cover letter be structured?
Keep it to one page, three to four short paragraphs. Open with a specific hook that
names the role and shows genuine interest in this company, not a generic "To whom it
may concern, I am writing to apply". In the middle, connect two or three of your most
relevant achievements directly to what the job needs, using concrete results rather than
restating your resume. Close by reaffirming your fit and interest and inviting a
conversation. Address it to a specific person where you can find the name.

## How do I tailor a cover letter to the job?
Mirror the language of the job description and reference something specific about the
company, such as a product, value, or recent development, to show you did your homework.
Pick the achievements that map most directly to the role's top requirements. Every letter
should read as if it could only have been written for that one job; a letter that could
be sent to any employer is a wasted opportunity and reviewers can tell.

## What makes a good networking or outreach message?
When contacting someone for a referral, informational chat, or to follow up, keep it
short, specific, and respectful of their time. Personalize the opening so it is clearly
not a mass message, state briefly who you are and why you are reaching out, make one
clear and easy-to-answer request, and thank them. For a referral, make it easy by
attaching your tailored resume and noting the exact role. People are far more willing to
help when the ask is small and specific.

## Should I follow up after applying or interviewing?
Yes. Send a brief thank-you email within a day of an interview, referencing something
specific from the conversation and reaffirming your interest, since this is expected and
leaves a good impression. After applying, a polite follow-up after one to two weeks is
reasonable if you have a contact, though avoid repeated messaging. Persistence that is
respectful signals genuine interest; persistence that is pushy does the opposite.
