# Writing Strong Resume Content

## How should I describe my work experience?
Lead each bullet with a strong action verb and focus on accomplishments, not duties.
Instead of "Responsible for managing social media", write "Grew Instagram following
42% in six months by launching a weekly content series". The pattern that works is
action + what you did + measurable result. Quantify wherever possible with numbers,
percentages, dollar amounts, time saved, or scale handled, because concrete figures
are more credible and memorable than vague claims. Keep each bullet to one or two
lines and start with the achievement, not the context.

## What should a professional summary contain?
A professional summary is two to four lines at the top of the resume that state who
you are professionally, your most relevant strengths, and the value you bring, using
keywords from the target role. It replaces the outdated "objective" statement. Tailor
it to each application so the first thing a recruiter reads matches the job. Skip
generic phrases like "hard-working team player" in favor of specific, evidence-backed
positioning such as "Data analyst with 4 years turning messy datasets into decisions
that cut reporting time by half".

## How long should my resume be?
For most people with under ten to fifteen years of experience, one page is ideal and
two pages is acceptable when you have enough relevant material to justify it. Recruiters
spend only seconds on an initial scan, so prioritize the most relevant and recent
experience and cut older or unrelated roles to bullet points or a single line. Length
matters less to an ATS than relevance and structure, but a focused, concise resume
serves the human reader far better.

## Should I tailor my resume for every job?
Yes. Tailoring is the single highest-leverage habit in a job search. Keep one master
resume with everything you have done, then for each application create a targeted
version that foregrounds the experience and keywords matching that specific posting.
This improves both your ATS keyword match and the human recruiter's sense that you fit
the role. It takes more time per application, but a smaller number of tailored
applications outperforms a large number of generic ones.

## What are common resume mistakes to avoid?
Avoid spelling and grammar errors, since an ATS will not correct them and a recruiter
reads them as carelessness. Avoid an unprofessional email address, listing every job
you have ever had, dense paragraphs instead of bullets, and including a photo or
personal details like age or marital status (common in some countries but a liability
with many ATS and in markets where it invites bias). Do not lie or exaggerate, because
claims are checked in interviews and references. Finally, do not rely on a functional
(skills-only, no dates) format, as newer ATS systems score it poorly and recruiters
distrust it.
